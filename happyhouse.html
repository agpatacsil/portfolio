Background

We've all seen the tragic site of the enormous pile of dishes in the sink. And there's always that one roommate that you have to constantly remind to pay their rent. When these little issues add up, they become increasingly frustrating causing unneeded tension and anger between roommates. Wanting to eliminate these unnecessary frustrations, my team went to the source of it. These frustrations seem to be rooted in poor household management, which led us to the question how do roommates organize and manage household tasks?

After speaking with different college students about their housing situations, management styles, and roommate stories, we wrote up our observations and created a list of user needs based off our interviews.

Observations from Interview One

  • some messages to roommates don't get noticed because they get mixed in with their other texts
  • hard to get all 5 people together for house meetings because they all have changing schedules
  • keep track of chores on a whiteboard and rotate them each week
  • a different person is in charge of each utility bill

Observations from Interview Two

  • people tend to break the clean your dishes within 24 hour rule
  • have a tally chart to add tally when you complete a chore
  • uses Splitwise and Venmo to calculate what everyone owes
  • only one roommate has contact with the landlord

Observations from Interview Three

  • keeps chore chart on the fridge because it's somewhere everyone frequents
  • don't assign chores, but rely on mutual understanding that everyone should do their fair share
  • use Facebook Messenger and text for communication
  • each person pays rent through online portal

Roommate needs . . .

  • a medium that has all aspects of roomming with people so they don't need to switch between multiple ones
  • a way to update schedules continuously in order to easily find a time that doesn't conflict with people's schedules
  • a way to keep track of photocopied bills
  • a way to easily split bills as multiple calculations can get confusing
  • a way of assigning distinct roles, so that there is no miscommunication as to who is in charge of what
  • a way to remind people to do their chores
  • a defined set of rules/steps, so that everyone is on the same page
  • a messaging app that makes their messages less accusatory and petty so it won't cause any fights or grudges
  • a way to locate where they store community supplies, so that everyone can use them
  • a way to notify their roommates that they completed a chore
  • a way of knowing how many supplies are left so they know if they need to buy more or not
  • a way of keeping each other informed

Point of View: Poor organization leads to dysfunctional management of a household. This is harmful because it causes unnecessary stress and conflict between roommates. This can be improved with better communication between roommates.

Storyboards

Instead of diving right into a specific user interface design, my team used our point of view as inspiration for creating three different design ideas that each address a user need. We illustrated these ideas with storyboards.

House Rules

Chores Reminder

Chores Tracker

House Rules: Different understandings of how to do a chore can lead to unnecessary arguments over wrongful completions of that chore. the house rules storyboard centers around this need for a defined set of rules/steps, so that everyone is on the same page. We liked this idea because it helps hold roommates more accountable for doing a chore correctly, and it simply helps define a list of house rules and steps for chores that can easily be accessed.

Chores Reminder: During our interviews, we found that some households use a tally chart to track how many times people have done chores, but don't assign specific chores to anyone. Since chores aren't assigned, this allows a lot of freedom and flexibility, which can be good, but can also be bad because you're relying on the idea that people will complete their fair share and will do so consistently. The dirty dishes storyboard includes a feature that helps assign specific chores to people and sends reminders, so that there is no confusion as to who is in charge of what and holds people accountable for doing their fair share.

Chores Tracker: The chore tracker storyboard was inspired by how house-related messages get lost or overlooked in the midst of other texts, Facebook messages, etc. We want to emphasize these messages by confining house-related chats to one app, so people don't have to mix house issues with other messages. We like this idea because it allows roommates to read a continuous house-related feed instead of having to scroll back through texts.

Response to Heuristic Evaluations

After discussing our observations from user testing and our results from heuristic evaluations, we listed out what changes we would make to improve each paper prototype before deciding which one to build as a web app.

Changes for Paper Prototype One

  • implement a "priority" option when making supplies, tasks, and chores
  • make a greater distinction between "to-do list" and "chores" tabs by having the "to-do list" in the user's profile
  • give the user the option to assign tasks to more than one person
  • use the same functionality as assigning and completing tasks for the deletion of multiple tasks
  • allow the user to have the option to log into the account instead of forcing the user to make an account first before using the app

Changes for Paper Prototype Two

  • use the open space in the home page to create a more user friendly menu, so that functionalities are more evident
  • make the ability to create a chore more evident because the current design is not intuitive
  • alter the process of creating a chore by taking out the need to click "manage group" before creating chore because it was confusing for users

Our next decision was really hard for us as we loved both of our paper prototypes. After further discussing evaluations, user testing, and what we would need to change, we decided on pursuing prototype one. We liked the visual qualities that prototype two offered, but we concluded that it wasn't as intuitive as prototype one and was way more complex. Our goal for our app was to give roommates an easy platform for household communication and prototype one provides all the necessary features while maintaining simplicity. Wireframes

Now that we had an idea of how our app will function, it was time to start thinking about how we will actually build it. To start this process, we created low-fidelity wireframes of our "home" and "tasks" pages, as they are both major screens in our app. These wireframes provide the layout of the screen and roughly what information it holds, so that we know what we need to code later on.

User Testing

After completing our wireframes, we began coding our working prototype (note that our main focus was to implement the described interactions, so it wasn't developed in a mobile format). Once our prototype was built, we went through user testing to learn what users were thinking while using our prototype and if their mental models matched ours. Ultimately we were looking for ways to improve our interface.

Before we started user testing, we developed a testing protocol to minimize observer bias. We were particularly interested in the user flow of creating, assigning, and completing a task, as this was the main feature that would enhance roommate communication. Below are our findings and the changes we made based off our testing.

Empty To Do List: In our original design, when users encountered the empty to do list, they either thought there was an error or the page was still loading. However, this was the correct page because there simply weren't tasks to show in their to do list. To mitigate confusion we added informational text telling the user that there were no tasks in their to do list.

Notifications Shortcut: In our original design, the user only had access to the notifications when they were on the home page. During user testing, only one person navigated to the notifications page, which includes various helpful assignment alerts and confirmation messages. It was common for users who didn't go to the notifications to voice their wish for alerts and messages, such as the ones provided by our notifications. Seeing the need to drive more traffic to the notifications, we added the notification bell to every page, so users would always have access to it, which will hopefully lead more users to check their notifications.

Directions: During testing, we found that it wasn't intuitive for users to click the checkboxes to assign a task, so we added directions in the new design that explicitly explain how to assign a task. Note that the directions will only appear when there are new tasks to assign.

Styled Checkbox: Users had a hard time distinguishing that the checkboxes were clickable in the original design, so we styled and made the checkboxes significantly larger. We hope that this styling makes it more intuitive to click the checkboxes.

Notifications Badge: In addition to making the notifications available on every page, we added a notifications badge for when the user has new alerts and messages. This is a way to keep users informed as before they had no way of knowing how many new messages they had unless they went to the notifications badge.

Completed vs History: In our original design, we had a "Completed" tab that included a full list of completed tasks, so that users have access to a record of what each other have done to make sure everyone is doing their fair share. However, naming the tab "Completed" led to navigational issues as users thought this tab was where they could complete a task. To mitigate this confusion, we changed the name of the tab to "History".

To Do List Shortcut: After assigning a task to themselves, the user is still in the "Tasks" page, so a lot of users navigated to the "in-progress" tab to complete a task as it is on the same page. This was a common mistake during testing and confusing for users as you can only view a task on the "in-progress" tab. In our new design, we hope to improve navigation by creating a link to the to-do list, which is where you can complete a task.

Color Coded List: During testing, users noted how they would like some way to differentiate task assignments in the "in-progress" tab, so that it's easier to grasp the info. In response, we color coded the "in-progress" list so that the current user's tasks are highlighted.

Overall, my team and I were really proud of the web app that we produced. It was extremely scary coming into this class and being told on the first day that we would need to produce a working prototype by week 10. It seemed slightly impossible in the beginning as we didn't even have an idea for what our app should be. However, I think everything worked out as my team and I found the inspiration for this app naturally as roommate conflicts is something that we've all had.

It was amazing getting to know my team better each week and by the end I was glad to call them my friends. Though we didn't always agree on every aspect of the project, we had such a high level of respect for each other making it easier to open up to each other's ideas. All in all, it was a great experience to get to go through the entire design process from needfinding all the way to prototypes and user testing, and it will be an experience that I will use to guide me in my future design projects.
